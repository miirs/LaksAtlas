Topic 01 · Anatomy

Fish Anatomy

External anatomy, tissues, organ systems and sense organs in teleost fish, primarily salmon.

Anatomical Terminology
Directions · planes · axes
▶

Precise anatomical language is essential for describing structures inside the fish body. All directional terms are relative to the fish's own orientation.

TermMeaningOpposite
Cranial / AnteriorToward the headCaudal / Posterior
Caudal / PosteriorToward the tailCranial / Anterior
DorsalToward the back (upper side)Ventral
VentralToward the belly (lower side)Dorsal
LateralToward the sideMedial
MedialToward the midlineLateral

Planes are imaginary flat surfaces used to describe where a cut would be made. The median plane divides the fish into two equal left and right halves, so there is only one. Sagittal planes run parallel to the median plane. Transverse planes cut perpendicular to the body axis, dividing into front and back sections. Horizontal planes divide into upper and lower sections.

External Anatomy
Body shape · fins · external landmarks
▶

Most fish are streamlined, with a conical head at the front and a body that tapers toward the tail. This body form reduces drag during swimming. Bottom-dwelling fish that move slowly tend to be less streamlined than fast pelagic swimmers.

Paired fins come in left-right pairs: pectoral fins sit just behind the gill cover, and pelvic fins are on the ventral side. The position of pelvic fins indicates evolutionary level. Far back (caudal) is primitive (as in salmon), further forward is advanced (as in wrasse).

Unpaired fins are single midline structures: dorsal fin on top, anal fin below toward the tail, and the caudal fin at the very end. Salmon also have a small fleshy adipose fin between the dorsal and caudal fins. It has no fin rays, no muscles, and no clear function.

On the head: mouth, eyes, nostrils (dorsally on the snout), and the operculum, the bony gill cover. The lateral line runs along the flank and detects water vibrations.

The "tail" in anatomical terms is the entire region behind the body cavity, not just the caudal fin.

Tissue Types
Epithelial · connective · muscle · nervous
▶

There are 4 main tissue types in all vertebrates. The study of tissues is called histology.

TissueKey featureWhere found
EpithelialCovers all inner and outer surfaces, no blood vessels, cells tightly packedSkin, gut lining, gills, kidney tubules
ConnectiveCells spread apart, defined by intercellular matrix (collagen, elastin, minerals)Bone, cartilage, blood, fat, loose/dense connective tissue
MuscleCells that contract, contain actin and myosinSwimming muscle, heart, gut wall, fins
NervousSpecialised for impulse conduction, long projections (axons, dendrites)Brain, spinal cord, all nerves

Epithelium is classified by layer count (single = simple, multiple = stratified) and cell shape (squamous = flat, cuboidal, columnar/cylindrical). The innermost lining of all blood vessels and the heart is a special single-layer squamous epithelium called endothelium.

Connective tissue subtypes: loose connective tissue fills spaces in organs, dense connective tissue forms skin dermis and swim bladder wall, cartilage (no blood vessels, cells = chondrocytes), bone (hardened with hydroxyapatite, cells = osteocytes), adipose tissue (fat cells), and blood (cells floating in plasma).

Nerve cells: conducting cells are neurons (cell body = perikaryon, input extensions = dendrites, output = axon/neurite). Non-conducting support cells are glial cells, which insulate, nourish and support neurons.

Skin and Scales
Epidermis · dermis · scale types
▶

Fish skin has two or three layers. Epidermis is the outermost: stratified squamous epithelium with numerous mucus cells (goblet cells). All fish are slimy. The mucus protects against infections and reduces friction. Unlike land vertebrates, fish epidermis has no dead outer layer and no blood vessels.

Dermis lies beneath the epidermis: dense connective tissue with regularly arranged collagen fibres running diagonally. The dermis contains blood vessels, nerves and pigment cells. Scales sit embedded in the dermis.

Hypodermis (when present) is the deepest layer: loose connective tissue and fat cells.

Fish scales are small bony plates:

Cycloid Smooth, rounded, primitive. Found in salmon. Growth rings allow age estimation.

Ctenoid Spiny rear edge (ctenii), more advanced. Found in wrasse. Some flatfish have cycloid on the underside and ctenoid on top.

Pigment cells in the skin are controlled by hormones (pituitary gland) and the nervous system, allowing fish to change colour, becoming lighter or darker.

Skeletal System
Cranium · vertebral column · fins
▶

The skeleton protects vital organs, stiffens the body, anchors muscles and drives movement. It is built of notochord tissue, cartilage and bone. Three main regions:

Cranium (skull): many bones, partially moveable relative to each other. Divided into the neurocranium (protects brain and sense organs) and the viscerocranium (jaws and gill apparatus). The teleost upper jaw includes a moveable premaxilla, a key defining feature of teleosts, allowing the mouth to be extended dramatically to capture prey. Fish have no breastbone (sternum).

Vertebral column (columna vertebralis): the main longitudinal stiffening. Each vertebra has a central body (corpus vertebralis, hourglass-shaped), a dorsal neural arch (the spinal cord passes through), and in the tail region a ventral haemal arch. Between vertebrae is a pad of notochord tissue that acts as an intervertebral disc, which is what makes the spine flexible and enables the S-curve swimming motion.

Fin skeleton: fins are stiffened by fin rays. Lepidotrichia (soft rays) are segmented, branched and paired, and flexible. Acanthia (spine rays) are unsegmented, unbranched and unpaired, and rigid, used as defence. The adipose fin is unique: it has neither rays, muscles nor fat, just connective tissue.

Muscle System
Red vs white · myotomes · jaw muscles
▶

The lateral swimming muscle is by far the largest muscle and accounts for most of the fish's economic value as food. In cross-section it has two clearly distinct zones:

Fibre typeLocationBlood supplyEnergy storeFunction
Red (aerobic)Outermost lateral stripRichFat + glycogenSustained, low-speed swimming (e.g. migration)
White (anaerobic)Bulk of the musclePoorVery littlePowerful burst swimming, fatigues quickly

Active swimmers like salmon, mackerel and herring have a higher proportion of red fibres than sedentary fish like pike, anglerfish or flatfish.

The lateral musculature is divided into myotomes, segments roughly equal in number to the vertebrae. Each myotome is W-shaped when seen from the side (with two peaks pointing caudally). The walls between segments are called myosepts. Muscle fibres inside a myotome are not parallel to the body axis, and their arrangement is complex and three-dimensional.

In the head, the dominant muscle is m. adductor mandibulae, the jaw-closing muscle. Additional muscles control the eyes, hyoid arch, operculum and gills.

Circulatory System
Heart · blood cells · vessels
▶

The fish heart has four chambers in series. Blood always flows in one direction:

Sinus venosus → Atrium → Ventricle → Bulbus arteriosus → gills → body → back to heart

The sinus venosus has a thin wall and receives four veins. The atrium is thin-walled with internal trabeculae. The ventricle is the main pump, and salmon have an additional outer compactum layer. The bulbus arteriosus is white, elastic, and smooths pulsatile pressure before blood reaches the gills. The heart is paced by cells in the sinus venosus and influenced by adrenaline and the nervous system.

Blood cells:

Cell typeFunctionKey feature
ErythrocytesOxygen transport (haemoglobin)Oval and nucleated, unlike mammalian RBCs. Produced in spleen and head kidney.
LymphocytesProduce antibodies (immune defence)Round, large dark nucleus
GranulocytesPhagocytosis, engulf pathogensBizarrely shaped nucleus (polymorphonuclear). Subtypes: eosinophil, neutrophil, basophil
MonocytesPhagocytosis, become macrophages in tissueLarge, bean-shaped nucleus
ThrombocytesBlood coagulationAnalogous to mammalian platelets

Main blood-producing (haematopoietic) organs: head kidney, spleen, and thymus.

🫁
Gills
Structure · gas exchange · ion regulation
▶

Teleosts have 4 gill arches per side. Each arch is a V-shaped structure of 4 bones with the tip pointing caudally. On each arch are two rows of gill filaments, flat, horizontal blades stiffened by cartilage. Both rows together form a holobranch, and one row alone is a hemibranch.

Each filament carries many vertical gill lamellae on both sides, the actual gas exchange surfaces. They are extremely thin, with blood flowing inside through channels formed by specialised pillar cells. Blood flows through the lamellae in the opposite direction to water flow, and this countercurrent arrangement maximises oxygen uptake.

At the base of the lamellae:

Mucus cells secrete protective mucus
Ionocytes (chloride cells) actively transport NaCl and other ions, key for osmoregulation in both freshwater and saltwater

Ventilation: Fish can use RAM ventilation (swimming with mouth open) or active opercular pumping. The operculum swings outward, drawing water in through the mouth, then the mouth closes and the operculum pushes inward, forcing water out over the gills.

The gills also excrete CO₂ and ammonia (the main nitrogenous waste in fish).

Digestive System
Gut tract · liver · pancreas
▶

The digestive tract runs from mouth to hindgut: oral cavity → pharynx → oesophagus → stomach → midgut → hindgut. Two accessory glands: liver and pancreas.

Oesophagus: short, muscular. Wall transitions from skeletal muscle (upper) to smooth muscle (lower). Lined with tall stratified squamous epithelium with many mucus cells.

Stomach: U-shaped in most fish. Lined with simple columnar epithelium. The surface layer is mucus cells, and deep gastric crypts contain chief cells that secrete HCl and pepsinogen. A muscular sphincter (pylorus) separates it from the midgut.

Pyloric caecae: finger-like blind pouches opening at the start of the midgut. They massively increase absorptive surface area. Salmon can have hundreds. Pancreatic tissue is typically found among them.

Midgut: nutrient absorption. Lined with tall columnar cells with a dense brush border (microvilli). Scattered mucus cells increase toward the hindgut.

Liver: the central metabolic organ. Located cranialy in the body cavity. Processes and stores nutrients, produces bile. Bile is stored in the gallbladder and released into the midgut. Salmon liver is reddish-brown with low fat, cod liver is large, pale and fat-rich.

Pancreas in teleosts is pancreas diffusum, not a compact organ but scattered cell clusters in mesenteries, along blood vessels, and in abdominal fat. Exocrine cells secrete digestive enzymes, and endocrine cells (Islets of Langerhans / Brockmann bodies) secrete hormones (e.g. insulin).

🎈
Swim Bladder
Buoyancy · physostomous vs physoclistous
▶

The swim bladder is a gas-filled sac lying dorsally in the body cavity just beneath the kidneys. Its function is buoyancy. A well-regulated swim bladder allows the fish to hover at depth without expending energy. However, the bladder sits slightly below the fish's centre of gravity, so an unconscious fish floats belly-up or on its side.

TypeDuct to oesophagus?WallExample
PhysostomousYes (open)Thin, low burst riskSalmon, herring
PhysoclistousNo (sealed)Thick dense connective tissue, withstands pressureCod, perch

Physoclistous fish regulate gas volume through a gas gland containing a rete mirabile, a countercurrent capillary network where lactic acid lowers pH, releasing oxygen from haemoglobin into the bladder. A separate oval region allows gas reabsorption.

🫘
Kidney
Nephron · urine production · functions
▶

Fish kidneys are always dark violet and always fixed dorsally to the vertebral column in the body cavity. They originally develop as one per side but fuse into a single organ. The kidney has three functions: producing urine, producing blood cells and producing hormones.

The functional unit is the nephron:

Glomerulus → Proximal tubule → Distal tubule → Collecting duct → Ureter

The glomerulus is a knot of capillaries wrapped by podocytes (specialised epithelial cells with finger-like processes). Blood is filtered here, and plasma is forced through as pre-urine (ultrafiltrate), while blood cells cannot pass. The tubules then modify the composition, reabsorbing useful ions and water, and secreting waste products, to produce the final urine.

Both ureters (one per kidney) merge into a single duct that leads to the urinary bladder, and urine exits separately at the urogenital papilla near the anal opening.

🧠
Nervous System
Brain regions · spinal cord · cranial nerves
▶

The nervous system divides into the central nervous system (CNS), made up of the brain and spinal cord, and the peripheral nervous system (PNS), made up of 10 pairs of cranial nerves and one pair of spinal nerves per vertebral segment.

The fish brain has 5 regions from front to back:

RegionKey structuresMain function
TelencephalonOlfactory bulb (bulbus olfactorius), limbic areasSmell processing, behaviour, learning
DiencephalonThalamus, hypothalamus, pineal gland, pituitary, saccus vasculosusIntegration, hormone control, circadian rhythm, seasonal sensing
MesencephalonOptic tectum (tectum opticum), two large lobesVisual processing (dominant sense in most fish)
MetencephalonCerebellumMotor coordination and balance
MyelencephalonMedulla oblongataCranial nerve nuclei, vital functions

The spinal cord runs through the neural arches of the vertebrae. Each spinal nerve enters/exits via two roots: dorsal root (sensory, carrying pain, temperature, touch) and ventral root (motor, to skeletal muscle).

The pineal gland senses light and produces melatonin to regulate circadian and seasonal rhythms, important for salmon smoltification and maturation timing.

👁️
Sense Organs
Eye · lateral line · inner ear · smell
▶

Fish have both general senses (pain, temperature, touch, located in the skin) and special senses with dedicated organs: smell, vision, taste, hearing/balance, lateral line, and electroreception.

Eye: same three-layer structure as all vertebrates (sclera → uvea → retina), but the lens is perfectly spherical (unlike our flattened lens). The outer sclera is cartilage, and the transparent cornea is dense connective tissue. The retina contains rod cells (low-light) and cone cells (colour). Visual signals travel via n. opticus to the optic tectum.

Inner ear: the sense of balance and hearing is located in the sacculus and utriculus, two fluid-filled sacs in the otic region of the skull. Each contains otoliths (ear stones of calcium carbonate) that press on hair cells when the fish accelerates or tilts. Three semicircular canals extending from the utriculus register rotation. Annual rings in otoliths reveal the fish's exact age.

Lateral line: a network of canals in the dermis running along the flank, above the eye, below the eye and along the jaw. Groups of hair cells deep in the canals detect low-frequency water vibrations and pressure changes, used to sense prey, predators and schooling neighbours. Hair cells are also the basis of electroreception.

Smell: olfactory pits on the dorsal snout. Most fish have two openings. Signals travel via n. olfactorius to the olfactory bulb (an anterior outgrowth of the telencephalon). Salmon famously use olfactory memory to navigate back to their natal river to spawn.

Taste: taste cells clustered in the oral cavity, throat, on the tongue and on barbels. Simple in structure, taste cell groups in epithelial knots.
