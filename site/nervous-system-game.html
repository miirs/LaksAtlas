The Nervous System

Neuron structure and signalling, the brain and cranial nerves, the spinal cord, and the autonomic nervous system in fish.

Neuron Structure & Function
Cell body · dendrites · axon · glial cells
▶

The nervous system is built from two cell types: neurons and glial cells. Neurons are excitable cells that rapidly receive, integrate and transmit electrical impulses called action potentials along their membrane. Glial cells do not conduct impulses. Instead, they insulate, nourish and support neurons.

A typical (motor) neuron has four functional zones: reception, integration, conduction and transmission.

Dendrites → Cell body (perikaryon) → Axon hillock → Axon → Synapse

Dendrites are short, branched projections covered in receptors. They sense incoming signals and convert them into electrical changes. The cell body (perikaryon) contains the nucleus, cytoplasm and the machinery for protein synthesis, and its membrane can also carry receptors. The axon hillock is the junction between cell body and axon, and is where incoming signals are summed and integrated. The axon is a long projection that carries the outgoing signal to the target cell.

Each axon is wrapped by insulating glial cells: oligodendrocytes in the central nervous system, Schwann cells in the peripheral nervous system. Where many layers of glial membrane wrap the axon, they form an insulating myelin sheath.

🔋
Ion Channels & the Resting Potential
Na⁺/K⁺ gradients · Na/K-ATPase
▶

At rest, a neuron's membrane potential sits at about −70 mV, meaning the inside of the cell is more negative than the outside. This resting potential is maintained by two things working together:

Passive diffusion: leak channels let potassium (K⁺) drift out of the cell and sodium (Na⁺) drift in, following their concentration gradients. At rest, there are more open K⁺ leak channels than Na⁺ leak channels, so the membrane is more permeable to potassium.

Active pumping: the enzyme Na/K-ATPase pumps 3 sodium ions out of the cell and 2 potassium ions in, against their gradients, using energy from ATP. This maintains the concentration gradients that make the resting potential possible.

Each ion has its own equilibrium potential, the voltage at which that ion would stop moving. For sodium this is about +60 mV, and for potassium about −90 mV. Ions always try to pull the membrane potential toward their own equilibrium potential whenever their channels are open, and this tug-of-war is what drives the action potential.

📈
The Action Potential
Threshold · depolarisation · repolarisation · refractory period
▶

An action potential is a rapid, stereotyped reversal of membrane polarity that travels along the axon without losing strength. It has three phases:

PhaseWhat happens
DepolarisationVoltage-gated Na⁺ channels open rapidly, Na⁺ floods in, making the inside more positive
RepolarisationNa⁺ channels inactivate, slower voltage-gated K⁺ channels open fully, K⁺ flows out, restoring negative charge
HyperpolarisationK⁺ channels are slow to close, so the membrane briefly overshoots to about −80 mV before settling back to rest

For an action potential to fire at all, incoming graded signals must sum to reach a threshold potential of around −50 mV. Below threshold nothing happens. Above it, Na⁺ channels open in a self-reinforcing cascade, an all-or-nothing event that is always the same size.

Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels have two gates: an activation gate and an inactivation gate. At rest the activation gate is closed. During depolarisation it opens, letting Na⁺ in. At the peak, the inactivation gate closes, shutting off further Na⁺ entry. Voltage-gated K⁺ channels are simpler, with just one gate.

Immediately after firing, a stretch of membrane cannot fire again. This is called the refractory period. The absolute refractory period lasts until the membrane returns to resting potential (Na⁺ channels are inactivated and unresponsive). The relative refractory period covers the hyperpolarisation phase, when a much stronger stimulus is needed to trigger a new action potential. This one-way "reset" is what forces the signal to travel in only one direction down the axon.

Information is encoded not in the size of action potentials (always identical) but in their frequency: stronger stimuli produce more frequent firing.

🧬
Myelination & Conduction Speed
Saltatory conduction · nodes of Ranvier · fibre types
▶

Myelin dramatically speeds up signal conduction. Between myelinated segments, small gaps called nodes of Ranvier expose the axon membrane, where voltage-gated ion channels are densely packed. Because myelin insulates the rest of the axon, an action potential at one node triggers a fast passive current that "jumps" to the next node, where a new action potential fires. This is called saltatory conduction, and it lets thin myelinated fibres conduct as fast as thick unmyelinated ones, without needing a huge nerve bundle.

In unmyelinated axons, the signal must regenerate continuously along the entire membrane, which is much slower.

Fibre typeMyelinated?DiameterConduction speed
Type AYes~10 µm~100 m/s (up to 120 m/s)
Type BYes (thinner)~5 µm~10 m/s
Type CNo<1 µm~1 m/s (max ~2 m/s)

Conduction speed is proportional to axon diameter, but scaling up diameter alone to increase speed would require impractically bulky nerves. Myelination solves this problem far more efficiently.

Synapses & Neurotransmitters
Chemical vs electrical · receptors · key transmitters
▶

Neurons mostly communicate via chemical synapses, the gap (synaptic cleft, ~20 nanometres) between the axon terminal and the target cell. Electrical synapses also exist, using gap junctions for direct electrical coupling, faster but less flexible.

Sequence at a chemical synapse: an action potential arrives at the axon terminal → voltage-gated calcium channels open → Ca²⁺ enters → this triggers synaptic vesicles to fuse with the membrane and release neurotransmitter by exocytosis → the neurotransmitter crosses the cleft and binds receptors on the target cell.

Receptors are either:

Ionotropic Part of an ion channel itself, fast, direct, brief effect (e.g. Na⁺/Ca²⁺ channels = excitatory, Cl⁻ channels = inhibitory)

Metabotropic Act indirectly via G-proteins and second messengers, slower, longer-lasting

Key neurotransmitters: glutamate is excitatory (opens cation channels, depolarises the cell). GABA is inhibitory (opens Cl⁻ channels, hyperpolarises the cell). Acetylcholine can be either. It excites skeletal muscle via nicotinic receptors, but can inhibit the heart via muscarinic receptors, depending which receptor the target cell expresses.

Transmitter action is brief (~1 millisecond). Molecules are cleared by enzymatic breakdown or reuptake into surrounding glial cells or the pre-synaptic neuron.

The Brain: Five Regions
Telencephalon → medulla oblongata
▶

The fish brain develops into five regions from front to back, each with distinct functions:

RegionKey structuresMain function
TelencephalonOlfactory bulb (lobus olfactorius)Receives smell input, not hollow in teleosts (unlike other vertebrates)
DiencephalonThalamus, hypothalamus, pineal gland, pituitary, saccus vasculosusThalamus = main sensory/motor relay, hypothalamus = homeostasis control centre
MesencephalonOptic tectum (tectum opticum)Primary visual processing, clearly layered, also a swimming-movement centre
MetencephalonCerebellumCoordinates movement, folded under the optic tectum (valvula cerebelli)
Medulla oblongataCranial nerve nucleiConnects to spinal cord via foramen magnum, houses major nerve nuclei incl. vagus

The pineal gland (corpus pineale) sits dorsally on the diencephalon and is directly light-sensitive. It produces melatonin and helps regulate daily and seasonal rhythms (relevant to salmon smoltification timing).

The optic nerve (n. opticus) crosses completely to the opposite side at the optic chiasm before reaching the tectum opticum. Fish have no stereoscopic vision, and each eye has a completely separate visual field.

Cerebellum cortex has three distinct layers: an outer molecular layer, a layer of large Purkinje cells, and an inner granular layer densely packed with cell bodies.

Cranial Nerves (I to X)
10 pairs · sensory, motor, or mixed
▶

Fish have 10 pairs of cranial nerves, numbered with Roman numerals. Each carries impulses to or from the brain (as opposed to spinal nerves, which serve the rest of the body).

NerveNameFunction
IOlfactoriusSmell, from olfactory pit to telencephalon
IIOpticusVision, crosses fully at optic chiasm to opposite tectum opticum
IIIOculomotorius4 of 6 eye muscles, parasympathetic fibres to the iris
IVTrochlearisOne eye movement muscle
VTrigeminusGeneral sense (touch, pain, temperature) from head skin and mouth, jaw-closing muscles
VIAbducensFinal eye movement muscle
VIIFacialisSensory from head lateral line, hyoid arch muscles
VIIIStatoacusticusBalance, from inner ear (utriculus, sacculus, lagena)
IXGlossopharyngeusTaste (rear mouth), first gill arch, sensory + motor
XVagusLargest cranial nerve, parasympathetic to heart, gut, smooth muscle, sensory + motor to remaining gill arches and lateral line

The vagus nerve is by far the most extensive. It supplies nearly all visceral organs and carries a large parasympathetic component, making it central to the autonomic nervous system.

🦴
Spinal Cord Organisation
Grey/white matter · dorsal & ventral roots · Mauthner cells
▶

The spinal cord (medulla spinalis) lies protected inside the neural arches of the vertebrae. In cross-section, cell bodies cluster in an inverted-Y shape called grey matter, and surrounding myelinated axon tracts form the lighter white matter.

RegionTypeCarries
Dorsal hornSensoryGeneral sense info (pain, temperature, touch) from skin, mostly reflex processing here
Ventral hornMotorSignals out to skeletal muscle

Each spinal nerve connects to the cord via two roots: radix dorsalis (dorsal root, sensory, cell bodies form the spinal ganglia) and radix ventralis (ventral root, motor).

Fish are considered more "spinal" than mammals. Many reflex behaviours are handled directly by the spinal cord without involving the brain.

Mauthner cells are a pair of exceptionally large neurons in the medulla oblongata (one per side), each with a thick, heavily myelinated axon running the full length of the spinal cord. They trigger the C-start escape response: a single firing event causes powerful, near-simultaneous contraction of lateral swimming muscle along one whole side of the body, letting the fish flee a threat almost instantly.

🔥
Autonomic Nervous System: Sympathetic
Stress · truncus sympaticus · noradrenaline
▶

The autonomic nervous system regulates homeostasis via involuntary control of heart muscle, smooth muscle and certain glands, coordinated from the hypothalamus. Unlike somatic motor neurons, autonomic pathways almost always use two neurons in series: a pre-ganglionic neuron (cell body inside the CNS) synapses onto a post-ganglionic neuron (cell body in a ganglion outside the CNS), which then reaches the target cell.

The sympathetic nervous system is active during stress and demanding situations ("fight or flight"). It stimulates the heart and redirects blood flow toward skeletal muscle and away from digestive organs.

Pre-ganglionic sympathetic neurons are short. They exit the spinal cord via the dorsal root and synapse quickly in a chain of ganglia lying just dorsal to the kidneys, called the truncus sympaticus (sympathetic trunk/chain), running nearly the full length of the spine. Post-ganglionic neurons are long, reaching all the way to organs and blood vessels. The transmitter released at the target is noradrenaline.

A special case: suprarenal cells (chromaffin cells) in the head kidney act as post-ganglionic neurons without their own axon. They receive direct pre-ganglionic input and release adrenaline and noradrenaline straight into the blood as hormones.

🍽️
Autonomic Nervous System: Parasympathetic
Rest & digest · vagus nerve dominance
▶

The parasympathetic nervous system governs digestion and energy storage, broadly the opposite role to the sympathetic system. Both systems are active simultaneously in most organs, forming a background balance called autonomic tone, which shifts as the fish's needs change.

The dominant parasympathetic nerve is the vagus (X), always well developed, targeting heart muscle and smooth muscle broadly. Unlike land vertebrates (which have parasympathetic supply to salivary and tear glands, absent in fish), fish parasympathetic fibres notably supply the intraocular iris muscles via the oculomotor nerve (III), and smooth muscle in the pharynx via nerves VII and IX.

Structurally, parasympathetic pathways are the reverse of sympathetic ones: long pre-ganglionic fibres travel almost all the way to the target organ before synapsing in a short post-ganglionic neuron very close to (or inside) the target tissue.
