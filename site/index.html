02 — Is It Getting Better?
Sea mortality rate has barely improved in 30 years
Despite industry promises of better welfare, the percentage of fish dying at sea has fluctuated between 13–22% since the mid-1990s. In 2024, 15.3% of all fish put to sea died — virtually unchanged from two decades ago.

03 — Scale of Death
More production, more death
As the industry has scaled from under 100 million to nearly 500 million fish put to sea, the absolute number of deaths has grown in lockstep. From 10 million fish dying in 1994 to 70 million in 2024.

04 — The Hidden Cost
170 million juveniles die before reaching the sea
The smolt and juvenile phase is rarely discussed. Yet in 2024, losses in land-based facilities were more than double the losses at sea. These fish die in hatcheries and smolt facilities from disease, handling, and production failures — invisible to the public.

05 — What Kills Them At Sea
Disease and wounds account for 84% of sea deaths
"Dødfisk" — fish that die from disease, wounds, and environmental stress — is by far the largest category at 59.1 million in 2024. Discarded fish (culled at slaughter), escapes, and other causes make up the rest.

06 — Disease & Sea Lice
ISA is rising. Lice violations spiked in 2024.
Infectious Salmon Anaemia (ISA) cases have climbed from single digits to over 20 per year. Pancreas Disease (PD) peaked at 159 cases in 2020. Meanwhile, lice limit violations nearly doubled from 556 in 2023 to 1,092 in 2024 — the worst since 2014.

07 — Regional Breakdown
A 5x difference in mortality between Norway's best and worst regions
Nordland produces the most salmon (360,000 tonnes) with the lowest mortality rate (7.1%). Finnmark loses 35% of all fish put to sea — five times worse. For every 1,000 tonnes produced, Finnmark loses 85,000 fish while Nordland loses just 21,000. But is this about farm density, or something else entirely?

What the Veterinary Institute Says
Key findings from Fiskehelserapporten 2024 (Veterinærinstituttet)

The Norwegian government has set a target to reduce mortality to 5% across all farmed species — roughly a third of the current level. The Veterinary Institute's disease database (covering 387 localities in 2024) reveals the main killers:

32.9%
Infectious diseases
26.6%
Injuries & trauma
21.2%
Unknown cause

The top three specific causes of death in 2024 were winter sores caused by the bacterium Moritella viscosa (13%), handling injuries during delousing operations (12%), and unknown causes (12%).

Finnmark's extreme mortality rate is partly explained by having very few active localities — individual catastrophic events disproportionately affect the regional statistics. A marine heatwave in late summer 2024 also caused additional mortality across multiple regions.

Source: Fiskehelserapporten 2024, Veterinærinstituttet (Rapport 1a – 2025)

08 — Cleaner Fish
Millions of fish used as living lice-removers
The industry deploys wrasse and lumpfish into salmon cages to eat sea lice. Usage peaked at 122 million in 2019 but has since declined to 45 million in 2024, partly due to high cleaner fish mortality and a shift toward mechanical delousing.
