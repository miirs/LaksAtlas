What's hiding
in the numbers

"Mortality" is only one slice of sea phase losses. Every year, millions of fish are recorded under a category called "Others" — and according to researchers, it largely consists of stock counting discrepancies that cannot be attributed to a specific cause. We do not know how many of those fish died, escaped, or were never there. That ambiguity is the problem.

The classification question

Norwegian law requires all removals from stock to be reported. But how they are categorised is a different matter. Companies report dead fish, escaped fish, culls, and "other" to the Directorate of Fisheries each month. The "other" category appears largely to consist of counting errors — both positive and negative — but it is unclear whether these relate to stocking figures, mortality, harvest counts, or escapes. As researcher Baard Misund (University of Stavanger) notes: "It is difficult to know what 'other' actually covers."

More subcategories are needed to provide sufficient precision. As it stands, it is also difficult to determine whether "Others" should be included in or excluded from mortality calculations — so different analysts make different choices, producing incomparable figures.

A welfare argument

The Norwegian government's own aquaculture white paper points to jellyfish and algae as reasons not to set a hard mortality limit — citing them as "unforeseen events" that would make a fixed threshold unfair to farmers. But jellyfish blooms and harmful algae are well-documented, recurring phenomena in Norwegian fjords. The industry has known about these risks for decades. If a farmer's fish die from a jellyfish bloom, that is still a production failure and a welfare failure.

Whether those deaths end up counted as "Mortality" or disappear into "Others" or counting discrepancies, the loss rate captures them either way. That is precisely why it is the more honest indicator than reported mortality alone.

03 — What is in "Others"
Largely counting errors — but we cannot be sure
According to research on Norwegian aquaculture statistics (Misund, University of Stavanger), the "Annet" category appears largely to consist of stock counting discrepancies — both positive and negative — rather than a specific cause of fish death. These could relate to errors in stocking figures, mortality counts, harvest tallies, or escape registrations. We simply do not know which. No further breakdown is published by Fiskeridirektoratet.
Stock counting errors
The most documented contributor. Companies count fish at stocking, during transfers, and at harvest. Discrepancies between expected and actual numbers are entered as "Other" — both positive (more fish than expected) and negative (fewer). The source of the error is not recorded.
Unattributed removals
Fish removed from stock that do not fit the reporting categories of dead, escaped, culled, or sold. This could include fish lost during transfers, wellboat incidents, or emergency removals where the cause is disputed or unclear at the time of reporting.
Jellyfish & algae events
Mass mortality from jellyfish blooms or harmful algae (HABs) may appear here or in Mortality depending on how the farm reports. The government's own white paper cites these as "unforeseen events" — but they are documented, recurring risks in Norwegian fjords, not genuine surprises.
Retroactive corrections
Because "Other" includes both positive and negative values, some entries represent corrections to earlier reports rather than new fish lost. This further blurs what the total figure actually represents over time.
Why this matters for welfare measurement

Because "Others" is undefined, analysts must decide whether to include or exclude it when calculating mortality rates. Different choices produce incomparable figures. The same farm, the same year, can look meaningfully different depending on which definition is used. As long as subcategories are not required, the "Others" figure tells us very little — except that something unaccounted for happened.

Fiskeridirektoratet itself acknowledges that more subcategories are needed. Until they exist, the total loss rate remains the most defensible metric: it counts every removal regardless of label.

What the stacked chart reveals

Reported mortality (Dødfisk) has grown in absolute terms as production scales up — but when viewed as a share of fish stocked, the picture is more nuanced. In 2005, 182.6 million fish were put to sea. By 2024 that was 461.9 million. Raw mortality counts are not directly comparable across years without accounting for production volume.

In peak "Others" years like 2011 (12.1M) and 2022 (8.8M), the unclassified category represents losses large enough to affect industry-level welfare statistics — yet their cause remains officially undocumented. Slaughterhouse rejects have held steady at 3–4 million per year: fish that completed their entire production cycle and still never became food.

The Norwegian government's target is 5% mortality per production cycle. Research shows only around 4–8% of completed cycles actually achieved this in the 2018–2022 period. That figure is based on reported Mortality (Dødfisk) alone. Add "Others" and the gap widens further.
